Your Assignment

You're a junior data scientist at St. Davids Health Consulting. It's 8 AM and your phone is already buzzing. A hospital client needs answers before noon - and Dr. Renata Voss, your VP, has dropped this in your lap.

The job is straightforward on paper: walk the full data science lifecycle from scratch. Define the question. Scope the data. Clean what needs cleaning. Explore what the data is actually saying. Interpret it carefully. Then communicate it to a room that includes clinicians, executives, and two trustees who have never touched a spreadsheet.

Each stage is a decision point. There are shortcuts you can take - and every one of them has consequences.

The DS Lifecycle

This simulation maps directly to the workflow from Module 1. At each stage, you'll face a realistic choice that tests whether you understand not just what to do, but why.

Learning Objectives

Define answerable questionsLearn the difference between vague curiosity and a precise, actionable analysis question.
Scope data requestsUnderstand why focused data acquisition is a skill - not just pulling everything you can find.
Handle missing dataMake cleaning decisions that preserve analytical integrity, not just row counts.
Spot confoundersPractice the critical thinking habit of asking "what third variable could explain this?" before drawing conclusions.
Communicate uncertaintyLearn to quantify and present confidence levels honestly to non-technical decision-makers.
Present to mixed audiencesUnderstand why the order and structure of findings matters as much as the findings themselves.

You started at St. Davids three months ago. You know pandas, you've cleaned a few datasets, and you've sat in on two client calls but never led one. This morning Dr. Voss stops at your desk on her way to the conference room. She sets her coffee down. That's never a good sign...

💌 Slack · Dr. Renata Voss · 8:02 AM

Good morning. I need you to take point on the Riverside ask today. They've seen a spike in 30-day readmissions this quarter - rate went from 12% to 17% - and the board wants to understand why before they meet at noon.

I know it's a fast turnaround. But I need someone who will do this right, not just fast. The last team that rushed question definition spent three days answering the wrong thing. Don't be that team.

Six stages. Don't skip any. I'll be in the board meeting - you'll brief me at 11:30.

You pull up the six-step framework from your onboarding notes. You've seen it on slides. Today you're actually using it.

The 6-Step Problem-Solving Framework - Module 1
1. Understand - Comprehend requirements and constraints before anything else
2. Plan - Map the logical flow before touching data
3. Break It Down - Divide into sub-problems; each one solvable independently
4. Pseudocode - Write out the logic before writing any code
5. Code Each Part - Implement piece by piece
6. Test & Debug - Check outputs, look for edge cases, verify conclusions

First step: understand. Which means before you open a single file, you need to know exactly what question you're trying to answer.

Stage 1 - Ask
Define the Question
8:14 AM · Your notebook

You resist the urge to open the data immediately. Dr. Voss's message is still on your screen: "Do this right, not just fast." You know from Module 1 that most analysis failures start here - not with the model, not with the data, but with the question.

The hospital said: "We want to know why readmissions are up." That's not an analysis question. That's a complaint. You need to turn it into something answerable.

From your Module 1 notes
Before touching data, you should be able to state: what decision will this analysis inform, and what would a good answer look like? If you can't answer those two questions, stop and ask.
